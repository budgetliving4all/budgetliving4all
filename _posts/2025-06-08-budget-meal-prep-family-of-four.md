Feeding a family of four on a budget while avoiding the nightly scramble for dinner requires a different approach than solo meal prep. The portions are larger, picky eaters complicate things, and you need more variety across the week or people stop eating what you prepared.

The structure that works most consistently is simpler than most family meal planning advice suggests: two proteins, one large grain, and three vegetables, all batch cooked on Sunday. From those components, you assemble different combinations through the week rather than reheating the same complete meal repeatedly.

## What a realistic family grocery budget looks like

Per [USDA ERS Food Price Outlook data](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings) and [BLS CPI food data](https://www.bls.gov/cpi/tables/supplemental-files/home.htm), food-at-home costs for a family of four vary widely by region. A moderate-cost plan for a family with two adults and two school-age children runs $250-300 per week at national averages. A thrifty plan runs $150-180.

The difference comes down almost entirely to protein choices and whether you are buying convenience items. Families who build meals around chicken thighs, ground beef, dried beans, and eggs rather than steak, deli meat, and pre-made items consistently spend significantly less.

Prices listed are national averages as of June 2025. Your costs will differ based on location, store, and current sales.


## The Sunday batch cooking structure

**Two proteins**

Cook one inexpensive animal protein and one plant protein each week. This gives flexibility for different meals and accommodates family members who eat differently.

Example week: 4 lbs bone-in chicken thighs (roasted) and 2 lbs dried black beans (slow cooked). The chicken works for rice bowls, tacos, pasta, and wraps. The beans work as a side, in tacos alongside or instead of the chicken, or in a simple soup with canned tomatoes and broth.

{% include safe-temp.html food="chicken" %}

**One large grain**

Four cups of dry rice yields roughly 12 cups cooked, enough for 4-5 dinners for a family of four as a side. Rice is the most cost-effective option. Brown rice costs around $1-2 per dry pound at most stores.

Pasta is a useful alternative when variety matters. One pound of dry pasta costs $1-2 and serves 4-6 as a main.

**Three vegetables**

Choose two that roast well (broccoli, cauliflower, sweet potato, cabbage, carrots, green beans) and one that works cold or at room temperature for lunches (cucumber, cherry tomatoes, shredded cabbage).

Roast in bulk at 400°F on sheet pans while the chicken is in the oven.

## The Sunday session: 2 hours for a family

The key is running everything at the same time.

**First 15 minutes**

Season chicken thighs and put them in the oven at 400°F. They need 40-45 minutes for a family-sized batch. Start the black beans in the slow cooker or a large pot (if from dried, they need 2-3 hours on the stovetop or 8 hours in the slow cooker). Start rice.

**Minutes 15-45**

While chicken and rice are cooking, prep vegetables. Toss broccoli and cubed sweet potatoes with olive oil and salt. When the chicken has 25 minutes left, add vegetable sheet pans to the oven.

**Minutes 45-90**

Chicken and vegetables come out. Check chicken with a thermometer.

Let everything cool for 15 minutes, then portion. For a family, large serving containers work better than individual portions. People can dish up different amounts at each meal.

**Minutes 90-120**

Label everything, refrigerate, transfer beans to the fridge once they finish. Per [USDA FSIS](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/leftovers-and-food-safety), refrigerate cooked food within 2 hours of cooking.

Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), cooked chicken and beans keep 3-4 days in the refrigerator. Cook enough for 4-5 days and freeze the rest immediately after portioning.


## Dealing with picky eaters

The component-based approach helps more than complete meals do. A child who will not eat a chicken and rice bowl will often eat plain chicken, plain rice, and plain broccoli served separately. Having components rather than assembled meals means each family member can put together what they will actually eat.

Keep one or two reliable crowd-pleasers in the rotation. Tacos made from the shredded chicken, ground beef cooked on a weeknight, or pasta with olive oil and parmesan are low-resistance meals that use the prepped ingredients without requiring everyone to eat the same thing.

## Scaling up the freezer

For families, a larger freezer operation makes sense. Double the batch and freeze half. A chest freezer ($150-200) pays for itself quickly in saved weeknight stress and buying proteins at sale prices in bulk.

The [freezer meal prep guide](/meal-prep/freezer-meal-prep-month/) covers the full approach for monthly batch cooking.


## Questions

### How do you keep meal prep from getting stale for a family?

Rotate one component each week. Same rice, same beans, different protein. Or same chicken, different grain (pasta instead of rice), different vegetables. Changing one element while keeping others constant reduces prep time while adding enough variety to avoid monotony.

### What is the cheapest protein to feed a family?

Dried beans and lentils remain the cheapest protein per gram at most stores. Bone-in chicken thighs are the most affordable animal protein at $1.50-2.50/lb currently. Ground beef has risen significantly. [Per USDA ERS, beef prices were 14.8% higher in April 2026 than April 2025](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings), but 80/20 ground beef bought in family packs remains a practical option.

### How do you handle different dietary needs within a family?

Building from components rather than complete dishes is the most practical solution. Cook proteins separately from grains and vegetables so each person can combine what works for them. One family member avoiding gluten does not affect rice and vegetable prep. A vegetarian in the household eats the beans and vegetables while others add chicken.
