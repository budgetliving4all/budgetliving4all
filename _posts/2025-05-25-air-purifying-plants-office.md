The 1989 NASA Clean Air Study tested 19 common houseplants for their ability to remove benzene, formaldehyde, and trichloroethylene from sealed chamber air. The [full study](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/19930073077) found that several common plants reduced these compounds meaningfully in a controlled environment. The [EPA notes](https://www.epa.gov/indoor-air-quality-iaq) that the effect in a typical office with ventilation is less dramatic than in a sealed chamber, but the plants do remove some pollutants and consistently improve air humidity.

The more practical benefit for most people is that plants reduce perceived stress and improve reported concentration in office environments, based on multiple workplace studies.

This guide focuses on plants that survive fluorescent light, require minimal care, and are non-toxic in case pets or children are nearby.


## The best options for a cubicle

**Snake plant (Sansevieria trifasciata)**

One of the most documented air-purifying plants in the NASA study, removing benzene, formaldehyde, trichloroethylene, xylene, and toluene. It tolerates low light including fluorescent-only environments, survives irregular watering, and only needs water every 2-6 weeks depending on the season. Water less in winter.

Non-toxic to humans but mildly toxic to cats and dogs if ingested.

Cost: $5-15 for a small pot at most garden centers or hardware stores.

**Pothos (Epipremnum aureum)**

Extremely difficult to kill. Tolerates low light, inconsistent watering, and poor soil. Trails nicely over a desk edge. Removed formaldehyde and carbon monoxide in NASA testing. Available in most grocery stores with garden sections for $3-8.

Toxic to cats, dogs, and children if ingested.

**Peace lily (Spathiphyllum)**

One of the top performers in the NASA study for removing multiple pollutants including ammonia. Tolerates low light. Droops visibly when it needs water, which makes the care schedule self-evident. Flowers occasionally in low-light conditions, which most other plants do not.

Toxic to cats, dogs, and children if ingested.

Cost: $10-20 for a medium plant.

**ZZ plant (Zamioculcas zamiifolia)**

Extremely drought-tolerant. Can go a month without water. Tolerates fluorescent light. Slower growing than pothos but very low maintenance. Good choice if you travel frequently or tend to forget plants.

Mildly toxic to humans, cats, and dogs if ingested.

Cost: $10-25 depending on size.

**Spider plant (Chlorophytum comosum)**

Non-toxic to cats, dogs, and humans, which makes it the safest choice if that matters for your situation. Tolerates low light. Produces small offshoots that can be propagated into new plants. Removed formaldehyde and xylene in NASA testing.

Cost: $5-10, or free if you know someone with one to propagate from.


## What does not work in a cubicle

**Succulents.** Commonly recommended but poorly suited for cubicles with no natural light. Most succulents need 6+ hours of direct or bright indirect light. Under fluorescent lighting they stretch toward the light source and deteriorate within weeks.

**Cacti.** Same issue as succulents. They need significant light and struggle under fluorescent-only conditions.

**Fiddle leaf figs and most large tropical plants.** They look good but require bright indirect light and consistent moisture. A cubicle is not the right environment.

**Orchids.** Need more light than fluorescent provides and are sensitive to overwatering.


## How to keep a cubicle plant alive

The most common cause of death for desk plants is overwatering, not underwatering. Most of the plants listed above prefer to dry out partially between waterings. A rough schedule:

- Snake plant and ZZ plant: water when the soil is completely dry 2 inches down, roughly every 2-4 weeks
- Pothos: water when the top inch of soil is dry, roughly every 1-2 weeks
- Peace lily: water when the soil is dry 1 inch down, or when it begins to droop slightly
- Spider plant: water when the top inch is dry, roughly every 1-2 weeks

Use a pot with drainage holes. Sitting in standing water is what kills most office plants. If your decorative pot does not have drainage, use a plain nursery pot inside it and empty the outer pot after watering.

Fluorescent lighting provides enough light for survival but not vigorous growth. If you want the plant to grow noticeably, a small grow light on a timer ($15-25) placed 6-12 inches above the plant makes a significant difference.


## Questions

### Do plants actually clean the air in a real office?

The NASA study results were from a sealed chamber, not a ventilated office. The [EPA acknowledges](https://www.epa.gov/indoor-air-quality-iaq) that plants remove some indoor air pollutants but notes the effect in a real ventilated space is smaller than in a sealed environment. You would need many plants to replicate the sealed-chamber effect. A few desk plants likely improve air quality modestly and humidity noticeably.

### Which plants are completely safe around pets and children?

Spider plants are the safest option among the commonly recommended low-light plants. They are non-toxic to cats, dogs, and humans per the ASPCA. Boston ferns are also non-toxic and tolerate low-to-medium light.

### How much does setting up a desk plant actually cost?

A small snake plant or pothos in a basic pot with suitable soil runs $8-15 for everything. That covers the plant, a clay pot with drainage, and a small bag of potting mix. It is a one-time cost and the plant will last years with minimal care.
