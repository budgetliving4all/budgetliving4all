The average American household throws away roughly 30-40% of the food it buys, per [USDA ERS food loss and waste research](https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/food-nutrition-assistance/food-security-in-the-us/food-loss-and-waste). On a $150 weekly grocery budget, that is $45-60 in the trash every week, or $2,300-3,100 per year.

Most of it is not spoiled food that could not be helped. It is produce bought without a plan, leftovers that never got eaten, and perfectly good food thrown out because of a misread date label.

The fixes are not complicated.


## Where the waste actually comes from

**Produce bought speculatively.** A bunch of kale because it looked fresh. A bag of limes for a recipe you might make. Strawberries because they were on sale. Without a specific plan for each item, a significant portion of fresh produce ends up in the bin.

**Leftovers that get pushed to the back.** Cooked food stored in opaque containers or behind other things in the fridge tends to get forgotten. Out of sight means out of mind and eventually past its safe eating window.

**Misread date labels.** Per [USDA FSIS](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/food-product-dating), "best if used by" and "use by" dates are manufacturer estimates of peak quality, not safety cutoffs — with the exception of infant formula. Food past a "best by" date is often still safe to eat. Throwing out a block of cheese or a container of yogurt because of a date that passed yesterday wastes money unnecessarily.

The USDA notes that "sell by" dates are for store inventory management, not consumer safety guidance. A carton of eggs marked "sell by June 10" is generally still safe to eat 3-5 weeks past that date if stored properly.

**Improper storage.** Some produce lasts much longer stored correctly. Some gets ruined within days if stored incorrectly.


## Storage that makes food last longer

Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts):

**Store these at room temperature, not in the fridge:**
Tomatoes (fridge kills the texture and flavor), potatoes, onions, garlic, and most whole citrus. Bananas slow their ripening at room temperature; the fridge turns the skin black, though the interior stays edible.

**Store these in the fridge:**
Cut fruit and vegetables, leafy greens, berries, dairy, eggs, and all cooked food.

**Leafy greens last longer dry.** Wash and spin or pat dry, then store in a container with a dry paper towel to absorb moisture. Wet greens rot faster.

**Herbs last longer in water.** Treat fresh herbs like flowers: trim the stems and stand them in a glass of water in the fridge. Cilantro, parsley, and basil stored this way last 1-2 weeks instead of 3-4 days.

**Cheese lasts longer wrapped properly.** Wrap hard cheeses in parchment or wax paper rather than plastic wrap. Plastic traps moisture and encourages mold. Parchment lets the cheese breathe.

**Freeze bread before it goes stale.** Bread goes from fresh to stale to moldy in 3-5 days at room temperature. Slice and freeze it when you know you will not finish the loaf, and toast slices directly from frozen.


## The practical habits that reduce waste most

**Buy produce with a plan for each item.** Before putting anything in the cart, know which meal it is for. This sounds obvious but most people browse produce by instinct rather than by need.

**Use a first-in-first-out system in the fridge.** New groceries go to the back. Older items come to the front. Takes ten seconds to implement and prevents the problem of things being pushed back and forgotten.

**Keep a "use it up" meal in your weekly rotation.** One night per week, cook whatever is closest to turning: the half-used bell pepper, the three remaining eggs, the wilting spinach. Eggs with whatever vegetables are around, a soup made from odds and ends, a grain bowl with leftover proteins. This habit alone eliminates most produce waste.

**Understand what "use by" actually means.** Before throwing something out because of a date, check whether it is a "use by" or "best by" label. Use your senses. Food that smells normal, looks normal, and has no visible mold is generally still safe, particularly for shelf-stable items. The [USDA FSIS guidance on food dating](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/food-product-dating) is specific about which labels are safety indicators and which are quality estimates.

**Freeze food before it goes bad, not after.** If you know you will not use something within its safe window, freeze it now. Bread, cheese, cooked grains, meat, and many vegetables freeze well. The [FoodSafety.gov Cold Food Storage Chart](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts) lists freezer times for most common foods.


## What to do with scraps

Vegetable scraps (onion ends, carrot peels, celery leaves, herb stems) can be simmered with water for 30-45 minutes to make a basic vegetable broth. Store in the freezer until you have enough scraps to make it worthwhile.

Chicken carcasses and pork bones from slow-cooked meals make flavorful stock with water, salt, and whatever aromatics you have. Cover with water, simmer 2-3 hours, strain, and refrigerate or freeze.

Stale bread makes breadcrumbs in a blender or food processor. Slightly overripe bananas are better for banana bread than fresh ones. Wilting vegetables work fine in soups, stews, and stir-fries where texture matters less.


## Questions

### Is food past its "sell by" date safe to eat?

Per [USDA FSIS](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/food-product-dating), "sell by" dates are for store inventory management and are not safety indicators. Food past a sell-by date is often still safe, provided it was stored correctly and shows no signs of spoilage. Use your judgment and your senses.

### How long do leftovers actually keep?

Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), cooked food keeps 3-4 days in the refrigerator at 40°F or below. Do not rely on smell alone to determine safety — some harmful bacteria do not affect the smell of food.

### What is the most commonly wasted food category?

Per USDA ERS research, fruits and vegetables account for the largest share of household food waste by weight, followed by dairy and meat. Targeting produce waste first has the biggest impact on both money and environmental footprint.
