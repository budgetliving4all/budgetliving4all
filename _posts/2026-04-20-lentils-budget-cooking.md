A pound of dried lentils costs $1.50-2.50 at most grocery stores and yields 6-8 servings of cooked lentils. Per [USDA FoodData Central](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/fdc-app.html#/food-details/172421/nutrients), one cup of cooked lentils provides 18 grams of protein and 16 grams of fiber. That works out to roughly $0.14-0.25 per serving of protein, cheaper than eggs, chicken, or any other common protein source.

They also cook in 20-25 minutes with no soaking required, which puts them ahead of dried beans for convenience.

The reason most people do not cook them more often is usually one bad experience with lentils that turned mushy or flavorless. Both problems are fixable.

## Types of lentils and what they are good for

**Brown and green lentils** hold their shape reasonably well after cooking and work in soups, stews, grain bowls, and salads. These are what most stores carry as a basic option. Cook time: 20-25 minutes.

**Red lentils** break down completely when cooked, producing a thick, creamy texture. They are better suited for soups, dals, and pureed dishes than for bowls where you want distinct pieces. Cook time: 15-20 minutes.

**French green lentils (Puy lentils)** hold their shape better than any other variety and have a slightly earthier flavor. More expensive, usually $3-5/lb, but worth it for salads and dishes where texture matters. Cook time: 25-30 minutes.

**Black lentils (Beluga)** hold their shape well and look striking in salads. Similar price to French green. Cook time: 25-30 minutes.

For budget cooking, brown or green lentils cover most uses and cost the least.


## How to cook lentils properly

The most common mistakes are adding salt too early (which toughens the skin) and not rinsing first (which can produce a muddy flavor).

**Basic method**

1. Measure 1 cup dried lentils. Pick out any debris or shriveled lentils.
2. Rinse thoroughly under cold water in a fine mesh strainer.
3. Add to a pot with 2.5 cups of cold water or broth. Broth adds significantly more flavor than water.
4. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to a low simmer.
5. Cover and cook 20-25 minutes for brown or green lentils. Check at 18 minutes.
6. Add salt in the last 5 minutes of cooking, not at the start.
7. Drain any remaining liquid.

The lentils are done when they are tender but still hold their shape. If they have started breaking apart, they are slightly overcooked. Still edible, but better suited for soups than bowls.

Lentils continue cooking from residual heat after you remove them from the stove. Take them off heat when they are just barely tender.


## Five practical ways to use cooked lentils

**Lentil and rice bowl.** Half a cup of cooked lentils over rice with olive oil, lemon juice, cumin, and whatever vegetables you have around. Add a soft-boiled egg if you want more protein. This is the fastest weekday meal once both components are prepped.

**Lentil soup.** Sauté onion, garlic, and carrots in olive oil until soft, add cooked lentils and a can of diced tomatoes, pour in 2 cups of broth, simmer 15 minutes. Season with cumin, salt, and a squeeze of lemon. Freezes well.

**Lentil tacos.** Cooked lentils seasoned with cumin, chili powder, garlic powder, and salt work as a meat substitute in tacos. Add a little tomato paste and a splash of water, cook until the mixture thickens. Not identical to ground beef but a practical and cheap alternative.

**Lentil salad.** Cooked and cooled lentils with diced cucumber, tomato, red onion, olive oil, red wine vinegar, and salt. Holds up in the fridge for 3 days without getting soggy, unlike most grain salads.

**Added to ground meat.** Stretching ground beef or turkey with an equal portion of cooked lentils reduces the meat cost by roughly half without a noticeable texture difference in dishes like bolognese, chili, or stuffed peppers. The lentils absorb the seasonings and fat from the meat.


## Batch cooking and storage

One cup of dried lentils is enough for 2-3 meals for one person. For a week of use, cook 2 cups dry (yields roughly 5 cups cooked) on Sunday.

Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), cooked lentils keep 3-5 days refrigerated in a sealed container. They also freeze well — portion into 1-cup servings in freezer bags, press flat, and freeze for up to 3 months.

Store dried lentils in a sealed container in a cool, dark place. They keep for 1-2 years, though older lentils may require slightly longer cooking time.


## Flavor

Plain lentils cooked in water are bland. That is true of most grains and legumes cooked in plain water. The fix is cooking them in broth instead of water, or building flavor into whatever you serve them with.

Lentils absorb flavors well from acids (lemon juice, vinegar), fats (olive oil, butter), and warm spices (cumin, coriander, turmeric, smoked paprika). A bowl of lentils with olive oil, lemon, and cumin tastes completely different from the same lentils with soy sauce, sesame oil, and ginger.


## Questions

### Do lentils need to be soaked before cooking?

No. Unlike most dried beans, lentils cook quickly without soaking. Rinsing them before cooking is sufficient. Soaking lentils does not meaningfully reduce cook time.

### Are canned lentils as good as dried?

Nutritionally, yes — per [USDA FoodData Central](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov), canned and dried lentils have similar nutrient profiles after cooking. Canned lentils cost roughly 3-4x more per serving and tend to be softer, which limits their use in dishes where texture matters. They are a good convenience option for quick meals.

### Why do my lentils turn to mush?

Overcooking. Red lentils turn to mush after about 20 minutes. That is their nature, and why they work well in soups. Brown and green lentils start breaking down around the 25-30 minute mark. Check them at 18-20 minutes and pull them off the heat when they are tender but intact.
