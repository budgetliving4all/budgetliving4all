If every meal prep guide you've found assumes you're cooking for a family of four, you're not alone. Most batch cooking advice is built around bulk quantities that either go bad before you eat them or leave you eating the exact same meal seven days in a row.

Meal prepping for one is a different skill — and once you get it right, it's one of the most powerful ways to cut your food bill without sacrificing the way you eat.

This guide covers exactly how to do it.


## Why meal prepping solo is actually easier (and cheaper)

Cooking for one person often feels wasteful — half a cabbage going soft in the fridge, a bunch of cilantro you'll never finish, chicken breasts you bought in a pack of six.

The problem isn't your portions. It's the system.

Solo meal prep fixes this by planning around *you* from the start. You buy what you'll eat, you cook what you bought, and nothing gets wasted. According to USDA data, the average American household throws away nearly 30% of the food it buys — a properly planned solo prep week can get that close to zero.

The other advantage: smaller batches mean faster cooking. A full solo prep session — proteins, grains, vegetables — typically takes 60–90 minutes on a Sunday.


## What a week of solo meal prep actually looks like

You don't need elaborate recipes. The most sustainable budget meal prep is built on a simple rotation:

- **One protein** (chicken thighs, ground turkey, eggs, canned tuna, lentils)
- **One grain** (rice, oats, pasta, quinoa)
- **Two vegetables** (whatever's cheapest that week — often broccoli, cabbage, carrots, or spinach)
- **A sauce or seasoning** to vary the flavour across the week

That combination gives you 10–14 mixed meals for the week. Swap the sauce on day 4 and it doesn't feel like leftovers.

## Step-by-step: how to prep a full week in 90 minutes

The key is cooking everything in parallel — not sequentially.

1. **Preheat oven to 400°F.** Season your chicken thighs with salt, pepper, garlic powder, and a splash of olive oil. They'll roast for 35–40 minutes while you do everything else.

2. **Start your rice.** 2 cups dry rice + 4 cups water in a pot. Bring to a boil, reduce heat, cover. Done in 35–40 minutes.

3. **Prep your vegetables.** Chop cabbage into wedges, toss frozen broccoli onto a sheet pan with olive oil and salt. Roast broccoli at the same time as the chicken (last 20 minutes).

4. **Cook your eggs.** Hard-boil 6 eggs for grab-and-go breakfasts. They keep in the shell in the fridge for up to a week.

5. **Portion everything.** Once cooked, divide your chicken, rice, and veg into individual containers. Label with the day or meal. Done.

## Common mistakes that make solo meal prep fail

**Prepping too much variety.** If you're making five different meals to keep things interesting, you've added 3x the cooking time and complexity. Start with one protein and two vegetables. Rotate flavours with different sauces — not different recipes.

**Buying fresh herbs you won't finish.** A bunch of parsley costs $1.50 and you'll use three sprigs. Buy dried herbs in bulk instead; they cost the same and last months.

**Ignoring the freezer.** A solo meal prep hack nobody talks about: when you cook a batch, freeze half of it in individual portions. Two weeks later you have a free "ready meal" that breaks the monotony.


## Frequently asked questions

### How much should I spend on meal prep for one person?

A realistic target is $30–$50 per week for all meals. You can get lower — around $25 — if you rely heavily on eggs, lentils, and oats. The $40–$50 range gives you more protein variety and the occasional treat without blowing the budget.

### How do I keep meal prep from getting boring?

The biggest lever is your sauce and seasoning, not your ingredients. The same chicken, rice, and broccoli tastes completely different with a soy-ginger glaze versus a lemon-herb marinade versus a smoky chipotle sauce. Keep 4–5 sauces in rotation and the base ingredients can stay the same all month.

### Can I meal prep without a lot of containers?

Yes — but invest in at least 5 airtight containers. You can also use zip-lock bags for portions you plan to freeze. The minimum setup: 5 meal containers (one per weekday), 2 larger containers for bulk-cooked grains and proteins in the fridge, and a few small containers for snacks.

### How long does prepped food last?

Cooked proteins and grains last 4–5 days in the fridge in sealed containers. Roasted vegetables last 3–4 days. Anything beyond that goes in the freezer immediately after cooking. Never let prepped food sit for more than 5 days before either eating or freezing it.


## Start this Sunday

The best time to start is your next free Sunday morning. You don't need a perfect plan — just pick one protein, one grain, and two vegetables, spend 90 minutes in the kitchen, and see what $15 of groceries can do for your week.

Once you've done it once, the whole process clicks.
