Most meal prep content assumes you're cooking for a family. The recipes feed six, the shopping lists reference bulk quantities, and the advice treats Sunday as a full day project.

If you're cooking for yourself, most of it doesn't apply. You end up with too much food, eating the same thing every day until you're sick of it, or throwing half of it away.

This guide is specifically for one person on a tight budget.


## The basic structure

The most manageable solo meal prep comes down to three things each week:

- One protein (chicken thighs, ground turkey, eggs, canned tuna, lentils)
- One grain (rice, pasta, oats, or quinoa)
- Two vegetables (whatever is on sale or cheapest that week)

Cook all three, portion them into containers, and you have the backbone of the week covered. Varying your sauces and seasonings helps, but it is not going to make the same food taste like something completely different. The more realistic goal is making the routine sustainable enough to actually repeat week after week. That matters more than making it exciting.

## The 90-minute Sunday routine

Everything cooks at the same time. That is what keeps it to 90 minutes.

Preheat the oven to 400°F. Season chicken thighs with salt, pepper, garlic powder, and olive oil and put them in. They take 35-40 minutes.

Start rice immediately after: two cups dry rice, four cups water, bring to a boil, reduce heat, cover. It takes about the same amount of time as the chicken.

While both are going, prep your vegetables. Cabbage wedges on one side of a sheet pan, frozen broccoli on the other, both tossed with olive oil and salt. The pan goes in the oven for the last 20 minutes of the chicken's cook time.

Hard-boil six eggs for breakfasts. They keep in the shell all week.

{% include safe-temp.html food="chicken" %}

Color is not a reliable way to tell if chicken is done. Use a food thermometer in the thickest part of the thigh, away from the bone. Pull it at 165°F.

Let everything cool for about 10 minutes, then divide into containers. Label by day or keep proteins and grains separate so you can mix portions through the week.

Active cooking time is around 20 minutes. The rest is the oven doing the work.


## How long it keeps

Per the [FoodSafety.gov Cold Food Storage Chart](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), cooked meat and poultry keeps 3 to 4 days refrigerated at 40°F or below. Cooked grains and roasted vegetables fall in the same range.

Your fridge should stay at or below 40°F. A fridge thermometer costs a few dollars and is worth having if you are not sure.

Anything you will not eat within 4 days should go into the freezer right after cooking, not after a few days in the fridge. Per [USDA FSIS guidance](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/leftovers-and-food-safety), refrigerate food within 2 hours of cooking, or 1 hour if your kitchen is above 90°F.

## What tends to go wrong

**Prepping too many different things.** Five different meals means five times the prep, more ingredients to buy, and usually something that never gets eaten. One protein and two vegetables is enough to start.

**Buying fresh herbs.** A bunch of parsley costs $1.50 and most of it will go bad. Dried herbs are cheaper and last months. Cilantro is the main exception if you use a lot of it.

**Not using the freezer.** Freeze half of each batch right after cooking. A few weeks in, you have ready meals that cost nothing extra and break up the repetition.


## Questions

### How much should I expect to spend?

$30-50 per week covers everything including breakfasts and lunches with reasonable protein variety. Closer to $25 is doable with eggs, lentils, and canned tuna. Closer to $50 gives you more options, like salmon or steak occasionally.

### What if eating similar food every week does not work for me?

Batch cooking is not the right fit for everyone. Some people do better prepping components separately, cooking rice and vegetables in advance but putting meals together fresh each day. It takes a few more minutes daily but feels less repetitive. Worth trying if the portioned container approach gets old quickly.

### What equipment do I actually need?

One sheet pan, one pot, five containers, and a food thermometer. That covers it.

The thermometer matters for food safety and costs $10-15.

### How do I know if something has gone bad?

Do not rely on smell or appearance alone. Some bacteria that cause food poisoning do not affect how food looks or smells. Follow the storage times above, and if something has been in the fridge more than 4 days, throw it out.


## Getting started

Pick a protein, a grain, and two vegetables. Block off 90 minutes this Sunday. Do one session and see how the week goes before deciding whether to adjust anything.
