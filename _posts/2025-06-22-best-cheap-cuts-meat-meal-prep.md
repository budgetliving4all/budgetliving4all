Meat is usually the most expensive item on a grocery list, and prices have been climbing. Per [USDA ERS Food Price Outlook data](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings), beef prices were 14.8% higher in April 2026 than in April 2025, and are projected to rise another 12.1% through 2026 as the U.S. cattle herd continues to shrink. Pork and poultry have been more stable, up 2.3% and 0.5% respectively over the same period.

That gap matters when you are planning a week of meals. The cuts below are consistently affordable, widely available, and practical for meal prep. They also happen to be some of the most flavorful options available.

## Chicken

Poultry is your best value protein right now. Prices have barely moved while beef has surged.

### Bone-in chicken thighs

The single most useful cut for budget meal prep. They cost less per pound than boneless thighs or chicken breasts, and the bone helps retain moisture during cooking, so they are harder to dry out. The fat content also gives them more flavor than breast meat.

Roast at 400°F for 35-40 minutes. Works in the oven, slow cooker, or braised on the stovetop. Pulls easily off the bone after cooking if you want boneless pieces for containers.

Typical price range: $1.50-2.50/lb bone-in, depending on region and store.

{% include safe-temp.html food="chicken" %}

### Chicken drumsticks

Often cheaper than thighs, sometimes as low as $0.99/lb on sale. Slightly more work to eat around the bone, which makes them less convenient for portioned containers, but they are a good option if you are cooking for a household and eating at the table rather than grabbing containers to go.

Same cooking method as thighs. Same temperature requirements.

### Whole chicken

Per pound, a whole chicken is usually the cheapest way to buy chicken. A 4-5 lb bird runs $5-8 and yields four to six servings of mixed pieces, plus a carcass you can simmer into broth. The trade-off is butchering it yourself or roasting it whole, which is not complicated but does take more time than throwing thighs on a sheet pan.

Rotisserie chickens from the grocery store deli are worth knowing about. Most stores sell them for $5-7, and a single bird provides enough meat for 3-4 meals for one person. Cheaper and faster than roasting yourself most of the time.


## Pork

Pork is significantly cheaper than beef right now and is underused in most meal prep plans.

### Pork shoulder (also sold as Boston butt or pork butt)

One of the best cuts for batch cooking. High in fat and connective tissue, which breaks down during long cooking and produces tender, shreddable meat. A 3-4 lb shoulder yields a large amount of food and works in tacos, rice bowls, sandwiches, and pasta.

Cook it low and slow: 300°F in the oven for 4-5 hours, or 8 hours on low in a slow cooker. It requires almost no active time and the result freezes well.

Typical price range: $1.50-2.50/lb.

{% include safe-temp.html food="pork" %}

### Bone-in pork chops

Cheaper than boneless and more flavorful. Works well pan-fried, baked, or grilled. Not as forgiving as shoulder if overcooked, so use a thermometer. Cuts thin at 3/4 inch take about 4-5 minutes per side in a hot pan. Thicker cuts do better finished in the oven after a quick sear.

Typical price range: $2.00-3.50/lb.

### Pork loin

Leaner than shoulder, closer in texture to chicken breast. A whole pork loin is one of the cheaper per-pound options at most stores and roasts easily in the oven. Slice into medallions for weekday meals or shred after slow cooking.

Typical price range: $2.00-3.00/lb on sale.


## Beef

Beef prices are at historic highs in 2026. That does not mean you cannot buy it, but it does mean being more selective about which cuts you choose and how you use them.

### Ground beef

The most versatile and widely available budget beef option. Works in dozens of dishes, cooks quickly, and takes up minimal freezer space when portioned flat. Buy 80/20 for the best balance of flavor and fat. Higher fat content means more flavor; 90/10 tends to cook dry unless you add moisture.

Buy the largest family pack your store carries. The per-pound price is almost always lower than smaller packages.

{% include safe-temp.html food="ground-beef" %}

### Chuck roast

Cut from the shoulder, chuck roast has significant connective tissue that breaks down during slow cooking into gelatin, which gives the meat a rich, almost silky texture. It does not work well cooked quickly. Low and slow is the only approach that produces good results: 275-300°F in the oven for 3-4 hours, or 8 hours on low in a slow cooker.

The result is shredded beef that works well in many dishes and freezes perfectly. A 3 lb roast will produce a significant amount of food.

Typical price range: $4.00-6.00/lb currently, reflecting the broader beef price increases.

{% include safe-temp.html food="beef" %}

### Bottom round roast

Leaner than chuck, which means less forgiving if overcooked. Works best roasted low and slow or sliced thin for stir-fries after marinating. Not as rich as chuck but noticeably cheaper at most stores. Worth considering if your budget is tight and you want beef in the rotation.


## What to skip

**Boneless skinless chicken breast at full price.** It is more expensive than thighs, dries out faster, and has less flavor. Fine when it is on sale and you specifically want lean protein, but it is not the best default choice.

**Pre-marinated or pre-seasoned meats.** You are paying for flavoring work you can do yourself in two minutes. The markup is significant.

**Stew meat in small packages.** Stores often sell pre-cut stew pieces at a higher per-pound rate than buying a chuck roast and cutting it yourself. Check the price per pound before buying.

**Pre-made meatballs, sausage patties, and similar processed items.** High markup, high sodium, and less flexible than buying ground meat and seasoning it yourself.


## Getting the most out of cheap cuts

**Buy in bulk when things go on sale.** Chicken thighs, ground beef, and pork shoulder all freeze raw with no quality loss. When a good sale price comes up, buy several pounds and freeze them flat in zip bags.

**Use the slow cooker for tough cuts.** Chuck roast and pork shoulder are both significantly cheaper than tender cuts and produce better results in a slow cooker than expensive steaks would. Long, low-temperature cooking is what makes them work.

**Keep bones for broth.** Chicken carcasses, pork bones, and beef bones can be simmered with water, salt, onion, and whatever vegetables you have around to produce stock. It costs almost nothing and keeps refrigerated for a week or frozen indefinitely.

**Learn to tell if something is actually a good deal.** Price per pound is the only number that matters. A $12 pork shoulder at 4 pounds is $3/lb. A $4 pack of chicken breast at 0.8 pounds is $5/lb. Check the label.


## Storage

Per the [FoodSafety.gov Cold Food Storage Chart](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts):

- Raw chicken: 1-2 days in the fridge, 9 months frozen
- Raw ground beef: 1-2 days in the fridge, 3-4 months frozen
- Raw pork chops: 3-5 days in the fridge, 4-6 months frozen
- Raw pork roast: 3-5 days in the fridge, 4-12 months frozen
- Cooked meat: 3-4 days in the fridge, 2-6 months frozen

If you are not cooking something within two days of buying it, freeze it.


## A thermometer

Every cut in this article requires one. Color is not a reliable way to tell if meat is done, and the consequence of undercooking is food poisoning. A thermometer costs $10-15 and lasts years.

## Questions

### Is cheaper meat lower quality?

Not necessarily. Chuck roast, pork shoulder, and chicken thighs are cheaper because they come from harder-working muscles that require longer cooking, not because they are inferior. Many cooks prefer them for flavor. The cuts that command high prices are priced that way because they are tender already and can be cooked quickly, which is a convenience premium, not a quality difference.

### Should I buy organic or grass-fed?

That is a personal decision. From a budget standpoint, conventional cuts are significantly cheaper and nutritionally similar. If organic or grass-fed matters to you for other reasons, that is worth factoring in, but it will raise your weekly food cost considerably.

### How do I know when a sale price is actually good?

Track your regular store's prices for a few weeks before deciding what counts as a good sale. Chicken thighs at $1.49/lb is genuinely good. At $2.49/lb it is probably just regular price. Most stores run the same cuts on sale on a predictable cycle, usually every 4-6 weeks.
