A stocked pantry changes how you shop and cook. Instead of buying everything fresh each week, you buy proteins and produce and fill gaps with what you already have. The weekly grocery bill goes down because the pantry is doing half the work.

The items below are chosen on three criteria: cost per use, shelf life, and how many different meals they support. Nothing on this list earns its place by being a specialty ingredient.

## Grains and starches

**Brown or white rice.** The most versatile and cost-effective grain. A 10 lb bag costs $8-12 and provides dozens of meals. Brown rice has more fiber and nutrients per [USDA FoodData Central](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov). White rice cooks faster and has a longer shelf life. Both work. Buy whichever your household prefers and will actually eat.

**Rolled oats.** A 42 oz canister runs $4-5 and covers a month of breakfasts. Oats also work in baked goods, as a binder in meatballs and burgers, and as a thickener in soups. Keeps 1-2 years stored in a sealed container.

**Dried pasta.** A pound of pasta costs $1-2 and serves 4 as a main. Buy a few shapes, one long and one short, and you cover most recipes. Keeps 2 years in a sealed container.

**All-purpose flour.** A 5 lb bag costs $3-4 and covers pancakes, simple breads, thickening sauces, and coating proteins before pan-frying. Keeps 1 year at room temperature, longer in the freezer.


## Dried and canned legumes

**Dried lentils.** The cheapest protein per gram of any food at most stores. A 1 lb bag costs $1.50-2.50 and yields 6-8 servings. No soaking required. Cook in 20-25 minutes. Per [USDA FoodData Central](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov), one cup cooked lentils provides 18g protein and 16g fiber.

**Dried black beans and pinto beans.** Require soaking overnight but cost half as much as canned. A 1 lb bag ($1.50-2) yields the equivalent of 3 cans. If time is a consistent constraint, canned beans at $0.80-1.25 per can are still a good value.

**Canned chickpeas.** More versatile than most canned legumes. Work cold in salads, roasted as a snack or topping, blended into hummus, and simmered in curries and soups. Stock 4-6 cans.


## Canned and jarred goods

**Canned diced tomatoes.** The foundation of dozens of soups, stews, chilis, and pasta sauces. Buy the store brand. A 14.5 oz can costs $0.75-1.25. Stock 8-12 cans.

**Canned tuna.** One of the cheapest sources of ready-to-eat protein. A 5 oz can provides 25g protein at roughly $1.25. Keep 6-8 cans in the pantry. See the protein cost breakdown in our [high protein meal prep guide](/meal-prep/high-protein-cheap-meal-prep/).

**Chicken or vegetable broth.** A 32 oz carton costs $2-4 and adds depth to rice, soups, and braised proteins that water alone cannot provide. Alternatively, make your own from vegetable scraps and chicken carcasses for nearly nothing.

**Canned coconut milk.** Adds creaminess to curries, soups, and rice dishes without dairy. Store brand runs $1.50-2.50 per can and keeps 2-3 years.


## Oils, acids, and condiments

**Neutral cooking oil.** Vegetable, canola, or sunflower oil for high-heat cooking. A 48 oz bottle costs $4-6 and lasts months. Per [USDA ERS price data](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings), vegetable oil prices rose significantly in 2022-2023 and have since stabilized.

**Olive oil.** For lower-heat cooking, finishing, and dressings. A decent 16 oz bottle costs $6-10. You do not need an expensive bottle for everyday cooking.

**Soy sauce.** Adds salt and umami to stir-fries, marinades, rice dishes, and soups. A 10 oz bottle costs $2-3 and lasts months. Low-sodium versions are widely available.

**Vinegar.** White vinegar for cleaning and basic cooking, apple cider vinegar or red wine vinegar for dressings and marinades. A bottle costs $2-4 and lasts a year or more.

**Hot sauce.** Personal preference, but a bottle of basic hot sauce ($3-4) adds flavor variety to simple meals at negligible cost per use.


## Spices

A core spice collection covers the majority of recipes across cuisines. Buy the largest containers your store carries. The per-ounce cost drops significantly in larger sizes.

- Salt and black pepper
- Garlic powder
- Onion powder
- Cumin
- Chili powder
- Smoked paprika
- Oregano
- Cinnamon
- Red pepper flakes

These nine cover Mexican, Mediterranean, American, and basic Asian-influenced cooking. Whole dried chiles, curry powder, and turmeric expand into more cuisines if you use them regularly.

Spices lose potency over time. Smell them before using. If there is no aroma, there will be no flavor contribution. Most ground spices are at their best within 1-2 years of opening.


## Sweeteners and baking basics

**Sugar.** A 4 lb bag costs $3-4 and covers baking, occasional sauces, and coffee. White sugar keeps indefinitely.

**Baking powder and baking soda.** Each costs $1-2 and keeps 6-12 months once opened. To check freshness, add a teaspoon to hot water. It should bubble actively.

**Peanut butter.** High protein, high calorie, cheap per serving, and shelf-stable. An 18 oz jar costs $3-4. Works for breakfast, snacks, and as a sauce base (thinned with soy sauce and a little vinegar for a peanut noodle sauce).

## What does not earn shelf space

**Specialty sauces and marinades.** Most bottled marinades cost $3-5 for a small amount and replicate what salt, acid, and a couple of spices do. Save the shelf space.

**Instant rice and instant oats in individual packets.** Both cost 3-5x more per serving than their bulk equivalents and provide no advantage in a kitchen with a pot and a kettle.

**Bread crumbs in a canister.** Make them from stale bread in 60 seconds in a blender.

**Pre-minced garlic in a jar.** A head of fresh garlic costs $0.50-1 and produces noticeably better flavor. Garlic powder from the spice collection covers recipes where fresh is inconvenient.


## Questions

### How much does it cost to stock a pantry from scratch?

A complete starting pantry covering all the categories above costs $60-90 upfront, depending on what you already have. Most of it lasts 6-12 months. The ongoing cost is restocking individual items as they run out, which adds $5-15 to the weekly grocery bill on average.

### What is the best way to store dry goods long-term?

Airtight containers in a cool, dark location. Heat, light, and moisture are the main causes of early spoilage for grains, dried beans, and spices. Glass jars with lids or plastic containers with tight-fitting lids both work. Keeping them visible makes it easier to track what needs restocking.

### What order should you stock a pantry if starting from nothing?

Start with the items that appear in the most recipes: rice or oats, dried lentils or canned beans, canned diced tomatoes, cooking oil, salt, garlic powder, and cumin. That covers a usable range of meals and costs under $20. Build out the rest over several weeks as the budget allows.
