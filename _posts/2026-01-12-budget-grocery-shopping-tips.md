Food-at-home prices were 2.3% higher in 2025 than 2024, per [USDA ERS](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-price-outlook/summary-findings). Beef prices rose 11.6% in 2025 alone. Egg prices, after years of HPAI-driven spikes, are finally coming down and are projected to fall 29.8% in 2026. The point is that grocery prices move around a lot and not always in the direction you want.

The strategies below work regardless of what prices are doing. They are about removing waste from your shopping habits, not about eating less or downgrading everything you buy.

## Shop with a list built from a meal plan

This is the single most effective change most people can make. Deciding what you are going to cook before you shop means you buy what you need and not much else. Shopping without a plan leads to buying things that look good in the store and then not using them, which is the primary source of food waste.

The meal plan does not need to be complicated. Write down 4-5 dinners for the week. List what you need for each. Check what you already have. Buy only what is missing.

Per [USDA ERS food spending data](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/ag-and-food-statistics-charting-the-essentials/food-prices-and-spending), U.S. households spent an average of 4.8% of disposable income on food at home in 2025. A household spending $200/week on groceries that eliminates 20% in unplanned purchases saves roughly $2,000 per year.


## Check the weekly circular before you shop

Every major grocery chain publishes a weekly ad with sale prices. Building your meal plan around what is on sale that week, particularly proteins, is one of the fastest ways to reduce costs without changing what you eat.

Most stores cycle the same items on sale every 4-6 weeks. Once you recognize the pattern, you can buy extra of things you use regularly when they hit their lowest price.

The store's app or website will have the current circular. Most also have a digital coupon section that loads discounts directly to your loyalty card with no clipping required.


## Buy store brands for staples

For most pantry staples, store brands are manufactured in the same facilities as name brands. Canned tomatoes, dry beans, rice, oats, frozen vegetables, and cooking oil are categories where brand name rarely makes a difference in quality but consistently makes a difference in price.

Categories where brand name does sometimes matter: certain condiments and sauces where the flavor profile is part of the product, and some baked goods. Everything else is worth trying in store brand.

The price difference is typically 20-40% per unit. On a $150 weekly grocery budget, switching staples to store brand saves $30-60 per week.


## Buy proteins in bulk when they go on sale and freeze them

Proteins are the most expensive item on most grocery bills and the most price-volatile. Chicken thighs, ground beef, and pork shoulder all freeze raw with no quality loss for months.

When your store runs bone-in chicken thighs at $0.99/lb or ground beef at $3.49/lb, buy as much as you can use in the next few months and freeze it flat in zip bags. You have now locked in a good price and removed proteins from your weekly budget until you need to restock.

Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), raw chicken keeps 9 months frozen and raw ground beef keeps 3-4 months. See our [cheap cuts of meat guide](/meal-prep/best-cheap-cuts-meat-meal-prep/) for which cuts freeze best.


## Know your price per unit, not your price per package

A 32 oz can of diced tomatoes for $2.49 is a better deal than a 14.5 oz can for $1.29. Most store shelf tags show price per ounce or price per unit. Use that number, not the package price.

This matters most for: canned goods, grains, pasta, dried beans, and cooking oil. The largest package available is almost always the cheapest per unit for items you use regularly.


## Buy produce that is versatile and seasonal

Produce prices fluctuate significantly with the season. Cabbage, carrots, frozen broccoli, frozen spinach, and sweet potatoes are cheap year-round. Most other vegetables spike in price during off-seasons.

Frozen vegetables are nutritionally equivalent to fresh per [USDA guidelines](https://www.dietaryguidelines.gov) and cost less per serving. They also produce no waste — you use what you need and the rest stays frozen. Fresh produce bought in bulk often goes bad before it gets used, which is expensive waste.

The most budget-efficient produce approach: one or two fresh items that are cheap right now, filled out with frozen vegetables for cooking.


## Avoid the things that reliably cost too much

**Pre-cut and pre-washed produce.** You are paying for labor. A head of cabbage takes 90 seconds to slice.

**Individual serving packages.** Single-serving oatmeal packets cost 3-5x more per ounce than oats in a canister. Same product.

**Deli counter meat by the pound.** Almost always more expensive than comparable packaged options. Useful for specific things, expensive as a staple.

**Bottled water.** If your tap water is drinkable, a filter pitcher costs $20-30 and removes the per-bottle cost entirely.

**Grocery delivery fees and tips.** Delivery adds 15-25% to a typical grocery bill between fees, tips, and the slightly higher item prices on many platforms. Worth evaluating whether the convenience justifies the cost.


## Questions

### Does couponing actually save significant money?

For most people, no. Digital coupons through store apps are worth using. They take 30 seconds and apply automatically. Traditional coupon clipping and extreme couponing requires significant time and tends to generate savings on processed and name-brand items you may not have bought otherwise. The strategies above save more money with less effort for most households.

### Is it cheaper to shop at discount stores like Aldi or Lidl?

Yes, consistently. Independent research and consumer comparisons consistently show Aldi and Lidl pricing 20-40% below mainstream supermarkets on comparable items. The trade-off is limited selection and fewer brand options. For staples and produce, they are worth using as a primary store if one is accessible.

### How much does meal planning actually save?

This varies widely by household, but studies on food waste suggest that the average U.S. household wastes roughly 30% of the food it buys. Even cutting that in half through better planning represents significant savings on any grocery budget.
