Getting enough protein without spending a lot requires knowing which foods deliver the most protein per dollar, not just per gram. A chicken breast is high in protein, but it costs significantly more per gram of protein than eggs or canned tuna.

This guide focuses on the cheapest sources, how to build a week of meals around them, and what daily protein targets actually mean for most people.

## How much protein you actually need

The [USDA Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2020-2025](https://www.dietaryguidelines.gov/sites/default/files/2020-12/Dietary_Guidelines_for_Americans_2020-2025.pdf) recommend 0.8 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day for sedentary adults. For a 150-pound (68 kg) person, that is roughly 54 grams per day.

Active people, those doing regular strength training, or older adults maintaining muscle mass need more. General recommendations for active individuals range from 1.2 to 1.6 grams per kilogram, which for a 150-pound person is 82-109 grams per day.

The 54-gram sedentary target is achievable on almost any diet. The 100+ gram target for active people requires more planning, but is still very achievable on a budget.

Lentils and eggs are the clear leaders on cost per gram of protein. The trade-off with plant proteins is lower leucine content, which affects muscle protein synthesis. For people actively trying to build or preserve muscle, animal proteins like eggs, chicken, and tuna are more efficient per gram. For general health and satiety, lentils and chickpeas work well.


## A week of high-protein meals on a budget

This plan targets 100+ grams of protein per day, appropriate for an active person around 150 pounds. Costs are based on May 2025 national averages.

**Breakfast (daily rotation)**
- 3 scrambled eggs + 1 cup oats with peanut butter: ~35g protein, ~$1.20
- Greek yogurt with cottage cheese: ~31g protein, ~$1.75
- 2 eggs + 1 can tuna: ~37g protein, ~$1.75

**Lunch (prepped Sunday)**
- Lentil and chicken bowl: 1/2 cup cooked lentils + 1 shredded chicken thigh over rice: ~35g protein, ~$1.50

**Dinner (prepped Sunday)**
- Ground beef and chickpea bowl: 4 oz ground beef + 1/2 cup chickpeas + roasted vegetables: ~33g protein, ~$2.50

Daily total: 100-105g protein at $4.70-5.95 per day. For a full week, $33-42 in groceries.


## The Sunday prep session

Proteins to batch cook for the week:

1. Roast 4-6 bone-in chicken thighs at 400°F for 35-40 minutes.

2. Brown 1 lb ground beef in a large pan.

3. Cook 1 cup dry lentils in 2.5 cups water, simmering 20-25 minutes until tender.

4. Hard-boil 12 eggs.

Portion everything into containers. The week's protein is now ready to assemble into meals in minutes.

Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), cooked meat keeps 3-4 days refrigerated. Hard-boiled eggs keep 1 week in the shell in the fridge. Freeze what you will not use within 4 days.


## What tends to go wrong

**Relying too heavily on protein powder.** Powder is expensive per gram compared to whole foods and does not provide the same satiety. Use it to fill gaps, not as a primary source.

**Buying chicken breast instead of thighs.** Breast costs roughly $4/lb versus $1.50-2/lb for bone-in thighs. The protein content per serving is similar. Thighs are harder to dry out and more forgiving for meal prep.

**Ignoring plant proteins.** Lentils and chickpeas add variety, cost very little, and provide fiber that purely animal-protein diets lack. Mixing plant and animal sources across the week is cheaper and nutritionally more complete.


## Questions

### How do I hit protein targets if I do not eat meat?

Eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, lentils, chickpeas, black beans, edamame, and tofu all provide substantial protein without meat. Combining legumes with eggs or dairy across the day reaches 100g targets without difficulty. The cost per gram of plant proteins is lower than most animal proteins.

### Does the quality of protein matter?

Protein quality is measured by amino acid profile and digestibility. Animal proteins are generally complete proteins with all essential amino acids. Most plant proteins are lower in one or more essential amino acids, which is why variety across the day matters for plant-based eaters. For most people eating a mix of sources, quality is not a practical concern.

### Is there a meaningful difference between fresh and canned tuna?

Nutritionally, canned tuna is comparable to fresh tuna per [USDA FoodData Central](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov). Canned tuna in water is a reliable, cheap source of protein with a long shelf life. Canned in oil provides more calories. The primary difference is taste and texture — canned is softer, which works well for mixed dishes and salads.
