A cast iron skillet is one of the few kitchen purchases where spending less often gets you a better product than spending more. The Lodge 10.25-inch has been recommended by New York Times Wirecutter, Serious Eats, Food Network, and TechGearLab as the best value cast iron available. The Victoria 10-inch is its closest competitor and a legitimate alternative.

Both are available for under $30. Both will outlast any nonstick pan you own. The differences between them are real but narrow.


## What cast iron actually does well

Before the comparison, a quick note on what cast iron is and is not good for.

Cast iron is excellent for: searing meat, pan-frying, cooking at high heat, going from stovetop to oven in one pan, browning proteins before slow cooking, and making cornbread or frittatas. Heat retention is its main advantage over other pans. It gets hot and stays hot, which is what produces a good sear.

Cast iron is not ideal for: cooking acidic foods like tomatoes or wine sauces for long periods (strips seasoning), delicate fish that breaks apart easily, or anyone who wants a pan they can toss in the dishwasher. It also takes longer to heat up than stainless or aluminum.

Maintenance requirement is real. After each use, wash with warm water and a stiff brush, dry immediately on the stove over low heat, and rub a thin coat of oil on the cooking surface. This takes about 3 minutes. Skip it regularly and the pan will rust.

## Which one to buy

**Buy Lodge if:** you want the most proven option with the largest review base, you need to pick it up at a store rather than ordering online, or you want the most widely available replacement parts and accessories.

**Buy Victoria if:** you want a smoother surface from day one, you find the Lodge handle uncomfortable, or you are buying it as a first cast iron for someone who wants slightly easier food release while building up seasoning.

Both are genuinely good pans. The Lodge is the safer recommendation because the data behind it is overwhelming. The Victoria is a real alternative, not a consolation pick.

What to avoid: any cast iron skillet priced above $80 in this size range unless you are specifically buying enameled cast iron (Le Creuset, Staub) for a different use case. The performance gap between a $30 Lodge and a $200 boutique skillet does not justify the price difference for most home cooks.


## Caring for cast iron

Both pans require the same care. After cooking, rinse with warm water while still warm, scrub with a stiff brush or chain mail scrubber, rinse, and dry immediately on the stovetop over low heat. While still warm, rub a very thin coat of a neutral oil (vegetable, canola, or shortening) over the cooking surface with a paper towel. The key word is thin. A thick coat of oil goes rancid and creates a sticky surface.

Do not soak in water. Do not put in the dishwasher. Do not let it air dry wet.

If rust appears, scrub it off with steel wool, re-season in the oven at 450°F for an hour with a thin coat of oil, and continue using normally.


## Questions

### Does cast iron work on induction cooktops?

Yes. Both Lodge and Victoria work on induction cooktops. Cast iron is magnetic and induction-compatible.

### How long does cast iron last?

Lodge pans from the 1950s are still in regular use. With basic maintenance, a cast iron skillet is effectively permanent. This is the main long-term cost argument for cast iron over nonstick pans that wear out in 2-5 years.

### Can you cook acidic foods in cast iron?

Briefly, yes. A quick tomato sauce or a splash of wine in a pan sauce will not damage a well-seasoned pan. Extended cooking of highly acidic foods strips seasoning over time and can impart a metallic flavor. For long-simmered tomato sauces, use enameled cast iron or stainless steel.
