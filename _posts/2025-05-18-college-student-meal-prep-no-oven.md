Most meal prep guides assume you have a full kitchen. If you are in a dorm or a small apartment with a no-cooking policy, or simply do not have access to an oven, those guides are not much help.

This covers what you can actually do with limited equipment and a limited budget.

## Equipment worth having

**Microwave.** Covers more cooking tasks than most people realize. Rice, scrambled eggs, steamed vegetables, reheating, and basic cooking for proteins like fish and chicken all work in a microwave with the right approach.

**Electric kettle.** Instant oats, instant noodles upgraded with real ingredients, couscous, and any recipe that calls for boiling water. A kettle is faster and more energy-efficient than heating water in a microwave.

**Rice cooker.** One of the most useful single appliances for this situation. Cooks rice reliably without attention, and most models also steam vegetables if you use the basket. A basic model runs $20-30 and pays for itself quickly in saved dining hall spending.

**Single electric hot plate.** If your housing allows it, a hot plate covers most stovetop tasks: scrambled eggs, sauteed vegetables, simple soups, pasta, and browning proteins. More versatile than any other single appliance.

**Blender or immersion blender.** Useful for protein smoothies and soups, not essential.


## Proteins that work without an oven

**Canned tuna and canned salmon.** No cooking required. High protein, cheap, and shelf-stable. Mixed with a little olive oil, lemon, salt, and whatever vegetables you have, it is a complete meal over rice or with crackers.

**Rotisserie chicken.** Most grocery stores sell them for $5-8. One bird yields 3-4 meals worth of protein for one person and requires no cooking. Shred it when you get home and portion into containers. See our [cheap cuts guide](/meal-prep/best-cheap-cuts-meat-meal-prep/) for more context on value.

**Eggs.** Scrambled eggs cook in 2 minutes in a microwave. Crack 3 eggs into a microwave-safe mug or bowl, whisk with a fork, add a pinch of salt, and microwave on high for 90 seconds, stirring at 45 seconds. The result is not as good as pan-scrambled but it is fast and fills the same nutritional role.

Per [USDA FSIS](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/microwave-ovens-and-food-safety), microwave cooking can heat unevenly. Stir food partway through cooking and check that it has reached a safe temperature throughout.

**Canned beans and lentils.** Already cooked, cheap, high in protein and fiber. Drain, rinse, heat in the microwave with a pinch of cumin and salt, and serve over rice.

**Greek yogurt and cottage cheese.** No cooking, high protein, works for breakfast or as a snack. Cottage cheese has become cheaper relative to other dairy products.


## A week of no-oven meals

All of these work with a microwave, rice cooker, and electric kettle.

**Breakfast**
- Instant oats made with hot water from the kettle, peanut butter, and a banana
- Microwave scrambled eggs with a can of black beans
- Greek yogurt with granola

**Lunch**
- Canned tuna over rice cooked in the rice cooker, with whatever vegetables you have
- Rotisserie chicken portions over rice with hot sauce
- Canned chickpeas heated in the microwave over rice with olive oil and salt

**Dinner**
- Ramen upgraded: cook instant noodles, drain most of the broth, add a soft-boiled egg, canned corn, and a portion of rotisserie chicken. Use half the seasoning packet.
- Rice cooker rice with canned beans and a can of diced tomatoes heated together in the microwave
- Microwave steamed broccoli (3 minutes in a covered bowl with 2 tablespoons of water) with rotisserie chicken and rice

**Cost estimate for one week**
Rotisserie chicken ($7), canned tuna 4 cans ($5), eggs 6-pack ($2), oats ($1.50), rice 2 lb ($2), canned beans 3 cans ($3.75), Greek yogurt ($3), bananas ($1.50), frozen broccoli bag ($1.50), peanut butter ($2.50). Total: approximately $30.


## Food safety in a dorm

Per [USDA FSIS guidelines on microwave cooking](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/microwave-ovens-and-food-safety), microwave ovens can cook unevenly, leaving cold spots where bacteria survive. Cover food, stir or rotate it during cooking, and allow standing time after cooking for heat to distribute.

Mini-fridges often run warmer than full-size refrigerators. Keep a refrigerator thermometer in your mini-fridge and aim for 40°F or below. Per [FoodSafety.gov](https://www.foodsafety.gov/food-safety-charts/cold-food-storage-charts), cooked food should not stay in the refrigerator beyond 3-4 days regardless of how it looks or smells.

Do not leave perishable food out for more than 2 hours.

## Questions

### Can you hard-boil eggs in a microwave?

Not safely. Eggs boiled in their shell in a microwave build up internal pressure and can explode. Microwave scrambled eggs and microwaved poached eggs (with a special poacher or in a shallow bowl of water) are both safe. Hard-boiled eggs require a stovetop or an electric egg cooker.

### Is a rice cooker actually worth it for a dorm?

For most people who cook rice more than twice a week, yes. It removes the guesswork, frees you up to do other things while it cooks, and most models keep rice warm for hours. Basic models are $20-30 and last for years.

### How do you keep food fresh with only a mini-fridge?

Prioritize shelf-stable items for most of your pantry: canned goods, oats, rice, peanut butter, crackers, dried pasta. Reserve the mini-fridge for dairy, eggs, opened cans, and prepped food. Use a thermometer to confirm the fridge is at 40°F or below.
